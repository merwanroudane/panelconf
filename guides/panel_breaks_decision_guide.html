2Is there cross-sectional dependence (CSD)?
Run the Pesaran CD test first. In macro panels the answer is almost always “yes”.
3Is the break sharp or gradual?
Sharp = a policy date / crisis. Gradual = slow structural change (technology, transition).
4Do all units break at the same date?
Same date = common break. Different dates = heterogeneous. In between = latent groups.
5Do all coefficients break together?
If you suspect only some effects moved, a vector break will over-split the rest.

Step 3 · Method profilesUse when / avoid when

🔲 Dummy family — sharp, dated breaks

xtpkpss

Panel KPSS stationarity with multiple breaks · Carrion et al. (2005)
✅ Use whenyou want the stationarity null (theory predicts convergence/PPP), breaks are sharp in level/trend, and each unit may have its own dates.
❌ Avoid whenbreaks are gradual, or you need the unit-root null.

xtlmbreak

LM cointegration, multiple breaks · Westerlund (2006)
✅ Use whenyou believe the equilibrium exists and want to test whether a break destroys it (H₀ = cointegration), with breaks in level/trend at different dates.
❌ Avoid whenyou want the "no cointegration" null, or breaks are smooth.

xtpcointegwe / xtpcointegboot

Westerlund–Edgerton (2008 factors / 2007 bootstrap)
✅ Use whenyou want cointegration with a level or regime shift and CSD removed by common factors (we), or a bootstrap when N is not huge (boot).
❌ Avoid whenthe break also hits the factor loadings — use xtcadfcoint.

xtbreakcoint / xtccecoint / xtcadfcoint

Banerjee–Carrion-i-Silvestre (2015 / 2017 / 2025)
✅ Use whenbreaks + CSD together and H₀ = no cointegration. Use the 2025 one (xtcadfcoint) when breaks may hit the deterministic part, the cointegrating vector and the loadings.
❌ Avoid whenT is very short — these are large-T tests.

〰️ Fourier family — smooth / unknown-timing breaks

xtpqroot, fourier

tFR: smooth + sharp breaks · Corakci & Omay (2023)
✅ Use whenyou suspect a gradual transition, or both gradual and abrupt at once, and you don't know the date or the number of breaks.
❌ Avoid whenyou have one clean, known policy date — a dummy is sharper.

xtfmg fccemg

Fourier CCE Mean Group · Guliyev (2026)
✅ Use whenbreaks occur at different dates in every unit — dummies would explode in number. Fourier terms absorb them while cross-section averages kill the common shock.
❌ Avoid whenyou need the exact break dates — Fourier does not report them.

xtpfardl

Fourier panel ARDL / CS-ARDL
✅ Use whenyour regressors are mixed I(0)/I(1) and you want the long-run + short-run coefficients with a smoothly shifting equilibrium.
❌ Avoid whenxtdcce2 is not installed (it is the engine).

xtnonlincoint

Nonlinear ECM / Fourier cointegration · Omay et al. (2017)
✅ Use whenadjustment back to equilibrium is asymmetric (faster above than below), or you want a Fourier smooth-break cointegration test.
❌ Avoid whenadjustment is plausibly linear — you lose power for nothing.

🎛️ Regularization family — let the data find the breaks

xtbreakmodel, method(pls)

Adaptive group fused lasso · Qian & Su (2016)
✅ Use whenyou expect a common break schedule and want the number and dates chosen automatically. Good default first try.
❌ Avoid whenunits clearly break at different dates → use gagfl.

xtbreakmodel, method(gagfl)

Grouped adaptive group fused lasso · Okui & Wang (2021)
✅ Use whenyou suspect latent groups — e.g. one cluster broke in 2008, another in 2011 — and you don't know who belongs where. It finds groups and dates.
❌ Avoid whenN is small (groups need members), or you need speed — it is the heaviest method.

xtcbc

Coefficient-by-coefficient breaks · Kaddoura (2025)
✅ Use whenyou suspect only some coefficients break. It leaves stable ones unbroken, keeping their standard errors small. Built for large N, small T.
❌ Avoid whentheory says everything shifts together at one regime change.

xtquantilebreak

Shrinkage quantile breaks · Zhang et al. (2022)
✅ Use whenthe effect may break differently in good vs bad times — i.e. across quantiles of the outcome.
❌ Avoid whenyou only care about the conditional mean.

🌐 Factor-aware estimators & specials

xtkpybreak

CCE with I(1) factors + breaks in slopes AND loadings · KPY (2011); BFW (2025)
✅ Use whenfactors are non-stationary (I(1)) and/or the factor loadings themselves may break. The most fully third-generation estimator here.
❌ Avoid whenbreaks are heterogeneous in timing — CCE needs a common break.

xtbfkbreak

Common break + CCE, endogenous regressors · Baltagi–Feng–Kao
✅ Use whena regressor is endogenous and you still need a common break with CSD removed. Syntax: xtbfkbreak y (x = z), breaks(1)
❌ Avoid whenyou have no valid instrument.

xtdynestimb

Dynamic panel robust to breaks and CSD
✅ Use whenthe model is dynamic (lagged y) and you need estimates robust to breaks + dependence. Nickell bias makes plain FE unsafe here.
❌ Avoid whenthe model is static — simpler tools are better.

xtpvarcoint

Panel VAR / VECM with cointegration and breaks
✅ Use whenyou want a system (all variables endogenous), impulse responses, or variance decomposition.
❌ Avoid whenyou have one clear dependent variable — a single equation is cheaper.

xtgets · xtfmg breaks

Break detection only
✅ Use whenyou want to discover which units broke and when before choosing a model. xtgets uses indicator saturation; xtfmg breaks a per-unit sup-Wald test.
❌ Avoid asyour final model — these are diagnostics, not estimators.

xtpcaus

Panel causality: Fourier Toda–Yamamoto & quantile causality
✅ Use whenthe question is "does x help predict y?" — PFTY is robust to unit roots and smooth breaks; PQC tests causality at different quantiles.
❌ Avoidinterpreting it as structural causality — it is predictive causality.

Step 5 · TrapsRed flags — the mistakes that get papers rejected

🚩 1. Reading the p-value without stating the null Half these tests have H₀ = cointegration (xtlmbreak, xtpcointegboot) and half H₀ = no cointegration (xtpcointegwe, xtbreakcoint). "Reject" means opposite things. This is the single most common error.
🚩 2. Using a first-generation test after the CD test rejected LLC/IPS/Pedroni assume independence. Under CSD they over-reject — you will "find" stationarity or cointegration that is an artefact of a shared global shock.
🚩 3. Ignoring a break in a unit-root test The Perron critique: a stationary series around a shifted mean looks like a unit root. You will wrongly conclude I(1) and difference away a real relationship.
🚩 4. A break date with no story If your estimated break is 1994 and nothing happened in 1994, suspect an artefact. Always report the date with a confidence interval and attribute it to a real event.
🚩 5. Forcing a vector break when only one coefficient moved It splits every coefficient at the union of dates, inflating variance and hiding significance. Kaddoura's crime example: vector-break finds 2 breaks in all 16 coefficients; CBC finds them in only 3. Use xtcbc.
🚩 6. Running a large-T test on T = 12 Unit-root and cointegration asymptotics rest on Brownian motion — they need a decent T (≥ 20–30). With short T use fixed-T methods (CBC, Baltagi–Feng–Kao).
✅ The defensible workflow CD test → detect/date breaks → unit root with breaks & CSD → number of factors → cointegration with breaks → estimate the break-dated coefficients → interpret each date against a real event.
