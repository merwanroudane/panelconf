Third-generation panel tests, with structural breaks

When your panel has both cross-sectional dependence and structural breaks, the tests you learned first will lie to you. This is the map of the estimators built to survive both — for unit roots and for cointegration — and the theory that makes them work.

The three generations

Each generation relaxes one more lie

Panel unit-root and cointegration tests are usually sorted into three "generations." The sorting is not about age — it is about which convenient assumption each one is finally willing to drop. Read them as a sequence of surrenders to reality.

First generation

Independent panels

Pool cross-sections assumed independent, no breaks. Power comes purely from stacking N series.

LLC · IPS · Breitung
Fisher-ADF/PP · Hadri
Second generation

Dependent panels

Units share common shocks (a factor structure). Tests defactor or augment with cross-section means — but still assume no breaks.

CIPS/CADF · PANIC
Moon–Perron · Choi
Third generation

Dependent panels + breaks

Common factors and structural breaks in level/trend, with break dates estimated, not assumed. This lecture.

Im–Lee–Tieslau · Carrion 2005
Bai–Carrion 2009 · Westerlund–Edgerton

The generations are cumulative. A 3ᵍ test does not abandon the factor machinery of 2ᵍ — it carries it and adds break-date estimation on top. If your data has factors but no breaks, a 2ᵍ test is more powerful; if it has breaks you ignore, a 2ᵍ test over-rejects or loses power badly.

The two problems

What cross-sectional dependence and a break each do to you

The generic panel model with a common-factor error is the frame everything below sits in. Write each series as a deterministic part, a common component, and an idiosyncratic part:

common-factor model yit = Dit + λi′Ft + eit

D — deterministic terms, where the breaks live (level shifts, trend breaks at dates TB).
λᵢ′Fₜ — r common factors loaded unit-specifically: this is the cross-sectional dependence.
eit — idiosyncratic error, unit-specific dynamics.

Cross-sectional dependence (CSD)

If Ft is present and you ignore it, the "independent-panels" asymptotics collapse: the pooled statistic no longer has the tabulated distribution, standard errors are wrong, and 1ᵍ tests severely over-reject a true unit-root null. Pesaran's insight (2ᵍ) — subtract the cross-section averages — soaks up a single common factor.

Structural breaks

A break in the deterministic part Dit mimics a unit root. A stationary series around a shifted mean looks persistent to an ADF regression that assumes a constant mean — the Perron critique, now at panel scale. Ignore the break and you lose power against stationarity; the test fails to reject a unit root that isn't there.

N individual series common factor Fₜ break date TB
Cross-sectional dependence
Structural break

Toggle the two problems on and off. With CSD on, the series move together — they share Ft. With the break on, every series jumps at TB: stationary noise around a shifted mean that a naïve ADF reads as a unit root.

Panel unit-root tests · 3ᵍ

Testing for a unit root when the panel breaks

Two families. LM / stationarity-based tests put the break under the null, so the break date does not distort size (Im–Lee–Tieslau; Carrion-i-Silvestre et al.). Factor-based tests separate common and idiosyncratic components first, then test each for a unit root with breaks allowed in both (Bai–Carrion-i-Silvestre). Watch the null direction — it flips between families.

Panel LM with level shifts

Im, Lee & Tieslau (2005, 2010) · "Panel LM unit-root tests with level breaks"

Score (LM) principle applied to each unit's break-augmented regression, then averaged across N. The LM statistic is invariant to the size and location of the level shifts under the null, so estimated break dates don't contaminate the distribution — its Achilles heel in earlier ADF-based break tests.

PANIC with structural breaks

Bai & Carrion-i-Silvestre (2009, RES) · "Structural changes, common stochastic trends & unit roots"

The 3ᵍ workhorse. Panel cointegration tests · 3ᵍ

Testing for a long-run relation that itself may shift

The cointegrating vector — the long-run equilibrium — can break too. A trade elasticity, a money-demand relation, an energy–growth link can shift level or slope at some date. 3ᵍ cointegration tests estimate that break date and remain valid under cross-sectional dependence, usually via bootstrap or a factor structure.

LM panel cointegration with breaks

Westerlund (2006) · "Testing for panel cointegration with multiple structural breaks"

An LM-based test whose null is cointegration. Allows the intercept and/or slope of the cointegrating relation to shift at multiple estimated dates, possibly at different dates across units. Cross-sectional dependence accommodated by a bootstrap that resamples the cross-section vector.

Westerlund (2006) and Westerlund–Edgerton test H₀ : cointegration (residual-based LM/KPSS). Gregory–Hansen, Westerlund (2007) ECM, and Banerjee–Carrion test H₀ : no cointegration. "Reject" means opposite things. Always state the null before you interpret the p-value — this is the single most common misreading in applied panels.

Theory & derivations

The four mechanisms that make 3ᵍ work

Everything above rests on four moving parts. Understand these and any specific test becomes a recombination of them.

1 · Defactoring — the PANIC decomposition

Start from yit = Dit + λi′Ft + eit. You cannot run principal components on the levels — if Ft and eit have different integration orders the estimator is inconsistent. PANIC's move is to difference first, extract factors from the stationary differences, then re-cumulate:

PANIC — differenced space Δyit = ΔDit + λi′ΔFt + Δeit
re-cumulate & test F̂t = Σt ΔF̂s  ·  êit = Σt Δêis

Now test F̂t and êit for unit roots separately. The idiosyncratic tests are pooled across i (they are asymptotically independent once the common factor is removed); the factors are tested with the MQ statistics for the number of common stochastic trends. Bai–Carrion adds break terms to Dit before differencing.

2 · Estimating the break date

The break date TB is a nuisance parameter estimated by grid search — pick the date that minimises the residual sum of squares (equivalently maximises the Wald/LM statistic for a shift):

break-date estimator T̂B = arg min TB∈[εT,(1−ε)T]  SSR(TB)

Trimming ε (typically 0.10–0.15) keeps the search away from the endpoints where a "break" is not identified. For multiple breaks, the dates are found by the Bai–Perron sequential / dynamic-programming algorithm and the number of breaks by an information criterion or a sequential sup-F(ℓ+1|ℓ) test. Because T̂B/T converges fast (super-consistently), the downstream unit-root statistic behaves as if the break date were known.

3 · Why LM tests put the break under the null

The Perron problem is that under the alternative (stationarity) an unmodelled break biases you toward the unit-root null. ADF-with-break tests specify the break only under the alternative, so critical values depend on the (estimated) break location — messy and size-distorting. LM / score tests specify the break under the null too, so the statistic is invariant to the break magnitude and its distribution does not depend on where the break is. That invariance is exactly why Im–Lee–Tieslau and Carrion et al. are trustworthy at panel scale.

4 · Pooling, bias-correction & the limit

Individual break-and-factor-adjusted statistics are averaged across N. Each has a non-zero mean and finite variance under the null (functionals of Brownian motion / Brownian bridges shifted by the deterministic terms). Standardise:

standardised panel statistic Z = √N · ( N−1Σi Si − μ ) / σ  →d  N(0,1)

The moments μ, σ² are the mean and variance of the individual limiting distribution — and they depend on the deterministic specification (constant vs trend, number of breaks). This is why every 3ᵍ paper tabulates a different (μ, σ²) for each model and each break count. Cross-sectional dependence, if not fully removed by factors, is handled by bootstrapping the cross-section vector so the resampled data keep the contemporaneous covariance while imposing the null.

The one sentence to remember

A third-generation test = remove the common factors (defactor or subtract cross-section means) + estimate the break dates by SSR grid search + test the cleaned series with critical values that know the deterministic model. Everything else is which piece each author emphasises.

Estimating the breaks · your Stata toolkit

From testing the break to estimating it

The tests above ask whether a series is I(1) or a relation cointegrates, allowing a break. The next question is estimation: recover the break dates and the regime-specific coefficients β(t) in a panel regression. This is a distinct, newer strand — the post-2016 panel structural-break estimators — and two of them are your own commands, xtbreakmodel and xtcbc.

Two different questions — don't conflate them

Integration/cointegration with breaks (previous sections) asks whether the series has a unit root or a stable long-run relation. Coefficient-break estimation (here) assumes the regression is well-specified and asks when the slopes shift and to what. In an applied paper you do the first to justify the model, then the second to estimate it.

The axis that separates them: how much break heterogeneity?

Every panel-break estimator makes an assumption about who breaks, and whether together. That single assumption is what distinguishes the methods — read the three regimes left to right as progressively more heterogeneity allowed.

Common vector breaks

all units · all coefs · same dates

Both coefficients jump at the same two dates. Fewest parameters, most power — if the assumption holds.

xtbreakmodel: pls · bfk · sara

Coefficient-by-coefficient

each coef · its own dates

One coefficient breaks early, the other late — or not at all. Avoids splitting regimes for coefficients that never moved.

xtcbc — CBCL (Kaddoura 2025)

Grouped heterogeneous

latent groups · group-specific dates

Units cluster into latent groups; each group has its own break dates and coefficients — membership estimated, not assumed.

xtbreakmodel: gagfl

Teaching contrast worth showing live: run pls and xtcbc on the same data. The vector method (pls) reports the union of all break dates and splits every coefficient there; xtcbc leaves genuinely stable coefficients unbroken. In Kaddoura's crime application, vector-break finds 2 breaks in all 16 coefficients while CBCL finds breaks in only 3 — the headline argument for coefficient-by-coefficient estimation.

Applied decision path

Choosing a test in front of your data

A defensible ordering for an empirical panel study — the sequence a referee will expect to see.

1

Test for cross-sectional dependence first

Pesaran CD test, Breusch–Pagan LM, Pesaran scaled LM. If CD rejects independence, 1ᵍ tests are off the table — you need at least 2ᵍ.

2

Test for and date the breaks

Bai–Perron on each series (or the panel deterministic part). If breaks are present, 2ᵍ is not enough either — move to 3ᵍ and let the test estimate TB.

3

Panel unit-root, break- and CSD-robust

Bai–Carrion PANIC (factor route) or Carrion et al. 2005 stationarity test (KPSS route). Report the null direction explicitly and the estimated break dates.

4

Estimate the number of common factors

Bai–Ng IC(p) / ICp2 criteria. This tells you whether the non-stationarity is a shared stochastic trend or idiosyncratic — a substantive economic result, not a nuisance.

5

If variables are I(1): cointegration with breaks

Westerlund–Edgerton (single break, simple) or Westerlund (2006) / Banerjee–Carrion (multiple breaks, factors). Match the break in the equilibrium to a real regime change.

6

Estimate the break-dated coefficients

Recover where the slopes shift and to what: xtbreakmodel (common or grouped vector breaks, four engines) or xtcbc (coefficient-by-coefficient, with csdemean for interactive effects). Match each estimated date to a real regime change.

7

Interpret the long-run relation

CUP-FM / CUP-BC, or Pesaran CS-ARDL / PMG allowing the estimated break. Read the adjustment speed and the shifted long-run coefficients against the dated regime change.
